#### **Mathematical Optimization and Positive Mappings (Purdue University)**

My most recent research experience was as a research assistant under Professor Thomas Sinclair at Purdue University, where I worked on a project investigating positive linear mappings and their extensions through a blend of theoretical analysis and computational experimentation. The central goal of the project was to understand when and how positive mappings can be extended or lifted, and how these properties can be characterized both algebraically and geometrically.

On the computational side, I developed Python-based tools to construct, classify, and analyze positive mappings under various constraints. I used linear programming to generate mappings that satisfied positivity and feasibility conditions, and applied support vector machines (SVMs) to efficiently classify large families of mappings based on their structural properties. This hybrid approach allowed us to move beyond purely theoretical examples and systematically explore the space of positive mappings at scale. I also implemented visualizations of the associated convex cones, which helped clarify geometric relationships that were difficult to see analytically.

In parallel, I engaged deeply with the theoretical foundations of the problem. I worked on proving properties of liftable and extendable mappings, including results showing that positive matrices are always extendable. The project required extensive use of convex analysis and optimization theory, particularly Farkas’ Lemma and its relationship to the hyperplane separation theorem, which strengthened my understanding of feasibility, duality, and constraint geometry. These theoretical insights directly informed the computational design of our algorithms and classification framework.

The results of this work were presented to an audience of approximately 40 students and faculty, and the full methodology and findings are being prepared for publication in a forthcoming research paper. Through this experience, I developed strong proficiency with NumPy, SciPy, and scikit-learn, while sharpening my ability to translate abstract mathematical concepts into implementable algorithms and interpretable visual outputs. Overall, this project deepened my appreciation for applied mathematics as a bridge between rigorous theory and scalable computation.

#### **Planetary Atmospheres and Martian Climate Dynamics (Purdue EAPS)**

My first research experience was as a research assistant in Purdue University’s Earth, Atmospheric, and Planetary Sciences (EAPS) department under Professor Lei Wang, where I studied Martian climate dynamics, with a particular focus on storm track variability and annular modes. This project centered on understanding how large-scale atmospheric patterns on Mars relate to dust activity, topography, and midlatitude dynamics.

My work involved analyzing large climate datasets, primarily EMARS and MACDA, stored in netCDF formats. Using Python and visualization tools such as Panoply, I examined spatial and temporal patterns in storm activity and investigated their connections to dust events and hemispheric asymmetries. I complemented this analysis with extensive literature reviews to contextualize Martian atmospheric behavior in relation to Earth and other planetary atmospheres.

A key focus of the project was understanding midlatitude eddy activity and its interaction with southern hemisphere topography on Mars. I also explored the role of annular modes in Martian atmospheric variability, drawing comparisons to analogous phenomena on Earth and Titan. To deepen my theoretical grounding, I worked with linearized equations for Rossby waves, linking wave propagation and instability to observed storm track behavior. Foundational texts such as Atmospheric Circulation of Exoplanets by Adam Showman helped situate these findings within a broader planetary science framework.

Throughout the project, I regularly presented findings during group meetings, contributed an abstract to the American Geophysical Union (AGU), and participated in a Geophysical Fluid Dynamics (GFD) bootcamp to strengthen my background in fluid dynamics and atmospheric modeling. This experience significantly enhanced my ability to work with large scientific datasets, apply mathematical modeling to physical systems, and communicate results within an interdisciplinary research environment.
